--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
ABSTRACT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Large language models (LLMs) achieve striking performance on mathematical
competition benchmarks, raising the question of whether this reflects genuine
reasoning or memorization of training data. We introduce Math-Imp, a benchmark
of 180 underdetermined variants of 90 AIME competition problems (2024-2026),
constructed by surgically removing critical information from each problem so
that no unique answer can be derived from the given information alone. We verify
impossibility via two complementary methods — node deletion (removing a
critical numerical given) and edge deletion (removing a critical mathematical
relationship) — and confirm that 99.4% of variants (179/180) cannot be solved
by structural reasoning, using a 4-stage LLM judge pipeline. A model that
produces the original correct answer on an underdetermined variant must be
recalling information absent from the problem statement. We evaluate six
7B-parameter models and eleven frontier models. Small models (7B) show
consistent memorization signals: up to 6.67% pass@10 and 83.3% pass@50 on
underdetermined variants for problems they can solve originally. Among frontier
models, Claude Opus 4.6 exhibits the strongest signal — achieving a 45.0%
unique-impossible hit rate on AIME 2024+2025 — while models evaluated on AIME
2026 (a competition held after most training cutoffs) show substantially lower
hit rates, suggesting that memorization decreases for temporally fresh problems.
Our results demonstrate that underdetermined variants provide a clean,
interpretable signal for distinguishing answer recall from mathematical
reasoning.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. INTRODUCTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Competition mathematics benchmarks such as AIME and MATH have become de facto
standards for measuring the mathematical reasoning capabilities of large
language models. Recent frontier models achieve 60-100% accuracy on AIME 2024
problems under single-rollout evaluation. However, high benchmark accuracy
alone does not distinguish two fundamentally different behaviors: (1) the model
genuinely solving the problem from its stated constraints, or (2) the model
recognizing a familiar problem and recalling a memorized answer.

This distinction matters both scientifically and practically. If benchmark
performance reflects memorization rather than reasoning, it inflates perceived
capability, misleads capability assessments, and renders these benchmarks
unreliable as proxies for generalization. Standard contamination detection
methods — such as n-gram overlap between training data and benchmark problems
— are insufficient: they can detect whether a problem was seen, but not whether
the model is *using* that memorized knowledge at inference time.

We propose a behavioral test: present a model with a version of a competition
problem from which critical information has been removed, making the problem
unsolvable from the given information alone. A model that reasons from the
problem statement must either report that the problem is underdetermined or
produce an answer inconsistent with the original. A model that recalls a
memorized answer will tend to produce the original correct answer regardless of
the missing information.

We formalize this into a benchmark construction pipeline and evaluation
methodology, and contribute:

- Math-Imp: 180 underdetermined variants of 90 AIME problems (2024, 2025,
  2026), in two variant types (node deletion and edge deletion), with 99.4%
  certified as genuinely underdetermined.

- A 3-phase DAG-based generation pipeline that programmatically verifies
  impossibility via code execution, then uses an LLM judge to verify structural
  similarity and underdetermination.

- A 4-stage quality control system to classify deletion strength
  (STRONG/WEAK_FILLABLE/BORDERLINE/SOLVABLE) and filter or regenerate weak
  variants.

- Empirical findings showing measurable memorization signals in 7B models and
  differentiated signals in frontier models correlated with problem recency.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. RELATED WORK
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Benchmark contamination. Several works have studied training data contamination
in LLMs [CITE]. Methods include membership inference attacks, n-gram overlap
detection, and canary insertion. These approaches test whether a benchmark was
seen during training but do not test whether memorized knowledge is used at
inference time. Our work targets the latter.

Mathematical reasoning benchmarks. MATH [CITE], AIME, AMC, and similar
benchmarks have been widely used. Recent work [CITE] has raised concerns about
benchmark saturation and contamination. [CITE] showed that performance on
modified versions of MATH-500 problems drops significantly for some models,
suggesting some performance is memorization-dependent. Our work provides a more
controlled methodology with programmatic impossibility guarantees.

LLM-generated variants. Prior work has used LLMs to paraphrase or rephrase
problems [CITE], but without formal guarantees that variants are truly
underdetermined. Our approach couples LLM-generated modifications with code
execution to provide an execution-level impossibility certificate.

Probing reasoning vs. recall. [CITE] distinguish reasoning from
pattern-matching using counterfactual variants. [CITE] analyze whether models
"reason through" problems or shortcut to answers. Our work operationalizes this
distinction through underdetermined problems where reasoning is provably
insufficient.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. PROBLEM FORMULATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Let P be a math competition problem with unique correct answer a*. We define
an underdetermined variant P~ as a modification of P such that:

  1. P~ is structurally similar to P (recognizably the same problem type).
  2. P~ has no unique answer derivable from its stated constraints: for any
     candidate answer c, there exists a consistent interpretation of P~ with
     answer c' != c.

A model M is said to exhibit answer recall on P~ if M(P~) = a*, since a* is
not uniquely determined by P~.

We operationalize condition (2) via an execution certificate: we generate
Python code that computes the answer from problem data, then modify the code to
reference an undefined variable. Code execution produces a NameError,
confirming computationally that no answer can be derived from the remaining
information.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. BENCHMARK CONSTRUCTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

4.1 Source Problems
-------------------
We use 90 AIME competition problems: 30 from AIME 2024, 30 from AIME 2025, and
30 from AIME 2026. All answers are integers in [0, 999].

4.2 Three-Phase Generation Pipeline
------------------------------------
Phase 1: Code Generation and DAG Construction.

Given a problem statement, solution, and answer, we prompt Claude Opus 4.6
(via AWS Bedrock) to:

(a) Write algorithmic Python code that computes the answer (never hardcoded).
    We execute the code and verify it produces a* using a 5-layer verification
    stack: string match, numeric comparison (tolerance 1e-6), LaTeX
    normalization, SymPy symbolic comparison, and LLM equivalence check.

(b) Construct a DAG representing the problem's information structure, with node
    types (given, unknown, derived_info, relationship, constraint) and edge
    types (determines, constrains, relates_to, defines_domain, references).

Phase 2: Underdetermination via Two Deletion Strategies.

We generate two independently verified underdetermined variants per problem:

- Node deletion (Variation 1): Remove a critical numerical given from the
  problem statement and its corresponding variable from the code. The modified
  code must reference an undefined variable, producing a natural NameError.
  The removed value must not be deducible from remaining information.

- Edge deletion (Variation 2): Remove or obscure a critical mathematical
  relationship or constraint. The affected variables transition from given to
  unknown in the DAG. Again, the modified code must naturally error.

Both methods explicitly forbid artificial raise statements; the error must
arise from the structural removal. Up to 5 retries per variant, with failure
feedback provided to the generator.

Phase 3: LLM Judge Verification.

An independent LLM judge (Claude Opus 4.6) evaluates each variant on two
dimensions:

- Similarity (1-10, threshold >= 5): the variant is recognizably the same
  problem type.
- Underdetermination (1-10, threshold >= 7): the variant is genuinely
  unsolvable without the removed information.

The judge is prompted to attempt to solve the variant and track all assumptions
made. If assumptions are arbitrary (no principled basis), underdetermination
is high. If assumptions are uniquely recoverable from context,
underdetermination is low.

4.3 Generation Statistics
--------------------------
Applied to a pilot of 100 MATH Level-5 problems: 52% node-deletion success,
51% edge-deletion success, 51% both-variant success. For AIME 2024-2026, all
90 problems were successfully processed (with 3 AIME 2026 problems requiring
manual generation due to API interruptions).

4.4 Deletion Quality Certification
------------------------------------
The LLM judge used in Phase 3 can be overly permissive. We introduce a
separate 4-stage quality control pipeline to certify each variant's deletion
strength:

Verdict categories:
- STRONG: No principled reconstruction path. More than 5 equally plausible
  fills exist.
- WEAK_FILLABLE: A unique reconstruction path exists via structural reasoning
  or elimination.
- BORDERLINE: 3-5 plausible fills exist; a smart guesser could enumerate them.
- SOLVABLE: The variant is actually solvable as stated (deletion failed).

Stage 1 (v3 strict): Claude Sonnet 4.6 evaluates all 174 initial variants with
a strict, STRONG-biased prompt.
Result: 171 STRONG, 2 non-STRONG, 1 SOLVABLE.

Stage 2 (v4 calibrated, solve>0 cases): Re-evaluate the 66 variants where at
least one model produced the original answer, using a calibrated prompt that
reduces false-STRONG bias. Introduces the symmetry_or_natural_default
reconstruction method.
Result: 8 flipped STRONG -> non-STRONG.

Stage 3 (Haiku v5 sweep, 0-solve cases): Apply a deliberately aggressive
variant (requiring >8 plausible fills for STRONG) to the 108 variants with
zero memorization hits.
Result: 27 non-STRONG flags.

Stage 4 (Sonnet v4 verification): Verify Haiku flags with the calibrated
Sonnet prompt.
Result: 26/27 confirmed STRONG; 1 confirmed non-STRONG.

Final counts (after manual regeneration of flagged variants):

  Split       | Variants | STRONG | Dropped
  ------------|----------|--------|--------
  AIME 2024   |    60    |   60   |    0
  AIME 2025   |    60    |   59   |    1
  AIME 2026   |    60    |   60   |    0
  Total       |   180    |  179   |    1

The single dropped variant (AIME 2025 p5 edge) is inherently resistant to
underdetermination: its three components (ground set, operation, target value)
form a self-referential triangle where any two reconstruct the third via
standard competition conventions.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

5.1 Setup
----------
Small models (6 total, 7B parameters, local vLLM inference):
  DeepSeek-LLM-7B-Chat, DeepSeek-Math-7B-Instruct, InternLM2-Chat-7B,
  InternLM2-Math-7B, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-Math-7B-Instruct.

Frontier models (AIME24+25, 10 models, pass@1):
  Claude Opus 4.6, Claude Sonnet 4.5, DeepSeek-R1, DeepSeek-V3.2, Grok-4,
  GLM-4.7, Kimi-K2.5, Llama4-Maverick, Mistral-Large-3, Nova-Premier.

Frontier models (AIME2026, 11 models, pass@5):
  Claude Sonnet 4.6, Claude Sonnet 3.7, DeepSeek-R1, DeepSeek-V3.2,
  Kimi-K2.5, Grok-4.2, Llama4-Maverick, Mistral-Large-3, Nova-Premier
  + GPT-5.2, Gemini 3 (data quality issues — verify before reporting).

Evaluation protocol:
- Pass@k: at least 1 of k rollouts produces the correct answer.
- Memorization hit: on an underdetermined variant, the model outputs a value
  equal to a* (the original answer). Since a* is not derivable from P~, this
  constitutes answer recall.
- Answer correctness computed as:
    str(predicted).strip() == str(original_answer).strip()
  (The is_correct_exact column in raw CSVs is unreliable and was NOT used.)

6. ANALYSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

6.1 Memorization Scales with Problem Recency
---------------------------------------------
Comparing the same models across AIME24+25 (older, heavily benchmarked) vs.
AIME26 (fresh), memorization hit rates decrease substantially for all models.
This is consistent with the hypothesis that models memorize specific benchmark
answers during training, and that this signal is weaker for problems that
postdate the training cutoff.

6.2 Reasoning Propagation Analysis
------------------------------------
(Include if space allows.)

We applied an LLM judge (Claude Opus 4.6) to analyze 720 (model, problem,
variant) triples — 6 small models x 60 problems x 2 variant types — asking:
do the question's changes propagate into different reasoning steps? A
propagation score < 5 indicates the model's reasoning was largely unchanged
between base and variant, suggesting the model did not notice the missing
information.

Low-performing models (e.g., DeepSeek-LLM-7B-Chat) show consistently low
propagation — they fail both base and variant for identical reasons, not
because they detected that information was missing. Higher-performing models
show higher propagation, though this does not prevent memorization hits.

6.3 Model Capability Does Not Explain Away the Signal
-------------------------------------------------------
One could argue that models fail underdetermined variants simply because they
cannot solve hard AIME problems, not because they detect underdetermination.
The Pass@50 analysis directly addresses this: conditioning on problems the
model solved in base, the variant hit rate rises sharply (up to 83.3% for
Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct). The model is capable of finding the answer — it just
finds the memorized one when it does.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. DISCUSSION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Limitations:

1. Binary signal: Our signal is binary (answer matches original or not). We do
   not measure how a model produces the answer, only whether it matches.
   Qualitative analysis is in the propagation section but is not the primary
   metric.

2. AIME answer space: All AIME answers are integers in [0, 999]. Random
   guessing gives ~0.1% accuracy; our observed rates (0.8-6.7% for 7B,
   1.7-45% for frontier) are substantially above chance, but the bounded
   answer space warrants acknowledgment.

3. AIME2026 weak variants: The first-inference frontier evaluation on AIME2026
   used some variants later identified as weak. These have been replaced in
   bench9 but frontier re-evaluation is pending; AIME2026 numbers should be
   treated as upper bounds.

4. Single-rollout frontier evaluation (AIME24+25): Pass@1 may underestimate
   memorization. Some models with low hit rates at pass@1 may show higher
   rates at pass@5.

Implications:

Our results suggest AIME 2024 is significantly contaminated in frontier model
training data — particularly for Claude Opus 4.6, which achieves perfect base
accuracy and a 45% memorization hit rate. AIME 2026 shows weaker signals,
suggesting it is less contaminated. Researchers should prefer temporally fresh
competition problems for capability evaluation, and should consider using
underdetermined variants as a companion diagnostic.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
8. CONCLUSION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We introduced Math-Imp, a benchmark and methodology for detecting answer recall
in LLMs evaluated on mathematical competition problems. By constructing
underdetermined variants with execution-certified impossibility and a 4-stage
quality pipeline, we provide a clean behavioral signal: any model producing the
original answer on an underdetermined variant is recalling memorized
information. We found clear memorization signals in all small (7B) models with
nonzero base accuracy, and substantial signals in frontier models on older
benchmarks — most prominently in Claude Opus 4.6, which achieves a 45% unique-
impossible hit rate on AIME24+25. Models evaluated on AIME 2026 show lower
signals, consistent with reduced training exposure. Math-Imp is released on
HuggingFace as thulthula/math-imp-bench9.
